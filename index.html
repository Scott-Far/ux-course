The problem with phases

Most UX curricula — and most UX teams — treat research and design as sequential phases. First you research, then you design, then you test. The deliverable at each stage signals completion: the research report, the wireframes, the test summary.

This model has a fatal flaw: by the time design starts, research is considered done. And by the time testing starts, designers have become attached to their solutions. Evidence from testing gets filtered through confirmation bias rather than used as genuine decision input.

"A deliverable marks the end of thinking. A decision marks the beginning of accountability."

The loop model

The research–design loop replaces linear phases with a continuous cycle where every design decision is traceable to a research question, and every research question is triggered by a design uncertainty. There is no "research is done" — there is only "we have enough confidence to decide."

Key principleThe loop is not a process to follow — it's a mindset to cultivate. You will use it even in 30-minute sprint sessions.

How this changes your practice

  • You stop producing research reports and start producing decision memos
  • You hold design critiques against research evidence, not visual preferences
  • You can answer the question "why did you make this decision?" with evidence, not intuition
  • Stakeholders stop asking for more data and start trusting your judgment

In the next lesson, we'll go deep on how to frame any project — no matter how vague the brief — as a tractable decision problem before you pick up a research method or open Figma.
