Module 1 — The UX Mindset

How to think like a UX practitioner

Ace Faranello

Lesson 1.1 — How to think like a UX practitioner

Video coming soon

By the end of this lesson you will be able to

  • Explain what the UX mindset actually means — beyond tools and methods
  • Describe why "faster horses" thinking limits good UX work
  • Apply the MacCready problem-reframing model to your own practice
  • Articulate the difference between producing deliverables and driving decisions

"Silicon Valley is a mindset, not a location." — Reid Hoffman

Let's begin with what every UX practitioner needs to understand deeply before anything else: how to think like one.

UX is a skill. It's also a science. It's also a way of seeing the world. It includes deciding where to place a button, understanding why people interact with that button the way they do, recognizing patterns and knowing why they work or don't. It includes the things most people entering the field already expect — interviewing, surveys, usability studies, wireframes.

But those are only one part of a much larger understanding of why this work exists at all.

When people new to UX say what draws them to it, they often talk about solving problems and creating experiences that feel wonderful. Many bootcamps are happy to oblige that framing — offering plenty of opportunity to design things and call it UX.

But this is where UX branches off from designing things to truly understanding why you're designing anything at all.

Becoming a UX practitioner requires identifying the right problems and their underlying causes. That's not something you can simply decide to do. It requires a way of thinking that allows you to see clearly enough to know what actually needs to be solved.

What this course goes after We will look not only at what is right in front of you, but at what might be there that a UX mindset allows you to see. Throughout this course, you will be challenged to look at the world from both perspectives.
A myth worth dispelling

The "faster horses" problem

If you've spent any time in tech or business, you've probably heard this quote:

"If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses." — attributed to Henry Ford

It implies that engaging with users to understand their needs is unnecessary — that people don't really know what they want. When I first heard it, something felt off. Henry Ford changed how human beings moved through the world. It seemed like there had to be more to the story.

There was. The quote appears to have first surfaced around 2001 in a letter to a UK marketing publication. The Henry Ford Museum investigated and found no evidence it ever appeared in Ford's letters, writings, or verified interviews. It is, in all likelihood, a fabrication — one that has been repeated so often it's become received wisdom.

More importantly, it turns out the quote originated in a marketing context. And marketing and UX look at customers very differently. Marketing views customers from the inside out — focused on acquisition and retention. UX views them from the outside in — focused on understanding the problems they face and finding solutions to address them.

"Faster horses" thinking undervalues the research, iteration, and trial and error that real solutions require. It also suggests that great design can happen in isolation, without ever engaging the people it's meant to serve.

Reflection

Where have you seen this before?

Think about a product, project, or team decision — one where someone assumed they already knew what users needed without asking. What happened? What was missed?

When facts ruin a good story

What was really happening in 1908

Let's take the quote at face value for a moment. If Ford really had said it, would it even hold up?

By 1908, hundreds of thousands of horses crowded city streets worldwide — and the problems they caused were well known and widely discussed. By 1894, London alone had over 50,000 horses in active daily use. The streets were dangerous, filthy, and difficult to move through. One reporter famously wrote that within fifty years, every street in London would be buried under nine feet of manure.

Horses were also deadly. By 1916, horse-related fatalities ran at nearly 17 per 10,000 horse-drawn vehicles — reportedly seven times Chicago's automobile fatality rate in 1997. City-dwelling horses collapsed on average once every hundred miles. When a 1,300-pound animal went down badly injured, it was shot on the spot and left in the street until the body rotted enough to be sawed apart and removed.

"The skittishness of horses added a dangerous level of unpredictability to nineteenth-century transportation. Horses often stampeded, but a more common danger came from horses kicking, biting, or trampling bystanders. Children were particularly at risk." — Eric Morris, From Horse Power to Horsepower

Given all of this, would Ford — a man immersed in these conditions — have assumed people simply wanted faster horses? Of course not. These problems were impossible to ignore.

And Ford didn't invent the automobile. That had been done a decade earlier by Daimler and Benz. What Ford did was look at the conditions people actually lived under — including wages of roughly 22 cents an hour — and design a manufacturing process that made a solution affordable at scale. The Model T outsold all other cars combined within six years of its introduction.

That's not ignoring users. That's deep, context-aware problem solving.

Despite all of this, the "faster horses" myth persists. It may be convenient — a way to rationalize skipping user engagement because it takes time and costs money. It may also reflect cultures where siloed teams find it easier to place blame than to confront what they didn't bother to learn.

Exercise

Find a real "faster horses" moment

Find one example — from a product you've used, a company you've followed, or your own experience — where a team made decisions without properly engaging users. It could be a failed launch, a confusing redesign, or a feature nobody uses. Write a brief description below, then answer: what would have changed if the team had approached it differently?

Understanding the problem

MacCready and the real question

"The problem is we don't understand the problem." — Paul MacCready

In 1977, Paul MacCready, an American aeronautical engineer, won the Kremer Prize — a monetary award for human-powered flight. Teams had been attempting this challenge for more than twenty years. All had failed.

The challenge was to build an aircraft powered solely by human muscle that could complete a figure-eight course covering one mile. Teams built beautifully crafted machines. They all crashed. And because rebuilding took months or years, each team managed only a handful of attempts.

MacCready didn't approach it the same way. Rather than assuming he understood the problem, he observed and researched until the real one became clear.

The other teams were trying to build a plane that wouldn't crash. MacCready asked a different question: what if he built a plane that could crash — and be rebuilt in hours instead of months? That single reframe changed everything. Where competitors iterated once a year, MacCready's team iterated daily. They failed faster, learned faster, and won.

His prize-winning aircraft, the Gossamer Albatross, succeeded not because it was the most sophisticated design — but because the problem it was solving was defined more accurately than anyone else had managed.

MacCready's approach is a recipe worth keeping:

  • Deep engagement with the real conditions — not assumptions about them
  • Close observation before committing to a solution
  • Identify root causes rather than surface symptoms
  • Form a hypothesis and test it
  • Fail, learn, and try again — faster than the competition

This is the core of the UX mindset. Not the tools. Not the deliverables. The thinking that makes them work.

The deliverable trap Most UX education teaches you to produce artifacts — personas, wireframes, journey maps, usability reports. These aren't wrong. But when the artifact becomes the goal, work stops when the document is finished. Research is "done" when the report is written. Design is "done" when the wireframes are approved. The question this course trains you to ask at every stage is not "what should I produce?" but "what decision does this work need to support?"
Reflection

Reframe a problem you've worked on

Think about a UX project or design challenge you've been part of — or observed from the outside. What was the stated problem? Now try applying MacCready's approach: was that actually the right problem to be solving? How might it have been reframed?

Wrapping up

What you now have

The UX mindset is not a checklist. It is a way of operating — one that prioritizes understanding problems over assuming them, and decisions over deliverables.

It is also, to be direct, not something most organizations are set up to support. You will encounter "faster horses" thinking regularly. Teams will rush to solutions. Stakeholders will ask for things that solve the wrong problem. Research will be skipped in the name of speed.

The UX mindset is what allows you to recognize those moments, name them clearly, and advocate for a better approach. Not just because it's the right thing to do — but because, as Ford and MacCready both demonstrated, it's what actually works.

In the next lesson, we'll look at the other side of this: what happens when teams do engage users, but fail to interpret what they're hearing correctly.
