Module 1 — The UX Mindset

How to think like a UX practitioner

Scott Faranello

Lesson 1.1 — How to think like a UX practitioner

Video coming soon

By the end of this lesson you will be able to

  • Explain what the UX mindset means beyond tools and methods
  • Describe why "faster horses" thinking limits good UX work
  • Begin seeing everyday problems the way a UX practitioner does

Let's begin with what every UX practitioner needs to understand deeply before anything else: how to think like one.

UX is a skill. It is also a science. It is also a way of seeing the world. It includes deciding where to place a button on a screen and understanding why people interact with that button the way they do. It includes recognizing patterns, knowing why they work, and knowing why they fail. It includes the things most people entering this field already expect: interviewing, surveys, usability studies, wireframes.

But those are only one part of a much larger understanding of why this work exists at all.

When people new to UX describe what draws them to it, they often talk about solving problems and creating experiences that feel intuitive or "delightful." Many bootcamps are happy to support that framing, offering plenty of opportunity to design things and call it UX.

But this is where UX branches off from designing things to truly understanding why you are designing anything at all.

Becoming a UX practitioner requires identifying the right problems and understanding their underlying causes. That is not something you can simply decide to do. It requires a way of thinking that allows you to see clearly enough to know what actually needs to be solved.

What this course goes after We will look not only at what is right in front of you, but at what a UX mindset allows you to see that others cannot. Throughout this course, you will be challenged to look at the world from both perspectives.

Good design may seem straightforward, and it is often presented that way. But like anything worth learning and doing well, it is never that simple.

A myth worth dispelling

The "faster horses" problem

If you have spent any time in technology or business, you have probably encountered this quote:

"If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses." — attributed to Henry Ford

It implies that engaging with users is unnecessary because people don't know what they want. When I first heard it, something felt off. Ford changed how human beings moved through the world. It seemed like there had to be more to this story.

There was.

The quote appears to have first surfaced around 2001, in a letter sent to a UK marketing publication. The Henry Ford Museum investigated this and found no evidence anywhere in Ford's letters, writings, or verified interviews. It is, in all likelihood, not something Ford ever said.

More importantly, the quote originated in a marketing context. And marketing and UX view customers very differently. Marketing views customers from the inside out, focused on acquisition and retention. UX views them from the outside in, focused on understanding the problems people face and finding solutions to address them.

Viewed through a marketing mindset, the quote suggests Ford simply knew what customers wanted. What it never explains is how he would know that.

"Faster horses" thinking undervalues the research, iteration, and trial and error that real solutions require. It also implies that great design can happen in isolation, without ever engaging the people it is meant to serve. And it allows teams to avoid the discomfort that genuine problem solving often requires.

When facts ruin a good story

What was really happening in 1908

Let's take the quote seriously for a moment. If Ford had actually said it, would it even hold up?

Ford introduced the Model T in 1908. Were people really looking for faster horses at the time, and if so, where did they need to go in such a hurry?

By 1894, London had over 50,000 horses in active daily use. City streets around the world were crowded, dangerous, and genuinely difficult to navigate. The problems caused by horse-powered transportation were not invisible or unknown. They were widely discussed, publicly documented, and impossible to ignore.

Horses were also extremely dangerous. City-dwelling horses collapsed on average once every hundred miles of travel. Horse-related fatalities ran at nearly 17 per 10,000 horse-drawn vehicles by 1916, a figure reportedly seven times higher than Chicago's automobile fatality rate in 1997. The conditions in these cities were a documented public health crisis.

Now place yourself in that world as a researcher and inventor. Would you look at all of that and conclude that people simply wanted faster horses?

Of course not.

Ford did not invent the automobile. That had been done a decade earlier. What Ford did was study the real conditions people lived under, including wages of roughly 22 cents an hour, and design a manufacturing process that made a solution to the very real horse problem affordable and accessible at scale. The Model T outsold every other automobile combined within six years of its introduction.

That is not ignoring users. That is context-aware, research-informed problem solving.

Despite all of this, the myth persists. It may serve as a convenient way to rationalize skipping user engagement because it takes time and costs money. It may also reflect organizational cultures where siloed teams find it easier to assign blame than to confront what they failed to learn.

"Faster horses" thinking does not just produce the wrong solution It produces the wrong solution with confidence, at scale, and at great expense. The UX mindset is the antidote.
Activity 1 — Noticing · ~5 minutes

Find a moment of friction in your own life

Think about something you use regularly. It could be an app, a household appliance, a form you filled out recently, your car, or anything at all. Now ask yourself: is there anything about using it that requires more thought than it should? A moment of hesitation, confusion, or a small workaround you have gotten used to?

Write down what you noticed. Don't try to solve it. Just describe what you observe and how it makes you feel when it happens. This is the beginning of the UX mindset: learning to notice before you interpret, and to observe before you solve.

Understanding the problem

MacCready and the real question

"The problem is we don't understand the problem." — Paul MacCready

In 1977, Paul MacCready, an American aeronautical engineer, won the Kremer Prize, a monetary award for human-powered flight. Design teams had been attempting this challenge for more than twenty years. All had failed.

The challenge was to build an aircraft powered solely by human muscle that could complete a figure-eight course covering one mile. Teams built carefully engineered machines. They all crashed. And because rebuilding took months or years, each team managed only a handful of attempts before running out of time or resources.

MacCready approached the problem differently. Rather than assuming he understood what needed to be solved, he spent time observing and researching until the real problem became clear.

Every other team was trying to build a plane that wouldn't crash. MacCready asked a different question: what if he built a plane that could crash, and be rebuilt in hours rather than months? That single reframe changed everything. Where competitors iterated once a year, MacCready's team iterated daily. They failed faster, learned faster, and won.

His prize-winning aircraft, the Gossamer Albatross, succeeded not because it was the most sophisticated design, but because the problem it was solving had been defined more accurately than anyone else had managed.

This is what the UX mindset is about at its core. Not the methods or the deliverables, but the thinking that makes them useful. MacCready's approach offers a recipe worth remembering and practicing:

  • Engage deeply with real conditions, not assumptions about them
  • Observe closely before committing to a solution
  • Identify root causes rather than surface symptoms
  • Form a hypothesis and test it
  • Fail, learn, and try again
The deliverable trap Most UX education teaches you to produce artifacts: personas, wireframes, journey maps, usability reports. That's not wrong. But when the artifact becomes the goal, work stops when it's finished. Research is done when the report is written. Design is done when the wireframes are approved. The question this course trains you to ask at every stage is not what should I produce, but what is the real problem, and what will my work help to solve.
Activity 2 — Reframing · ~5 minutes

Ask MacCready's question

Go back to what you noticed in Activity 1. Now look at it and ask MacCready's question: what is the real problem here?

For example, if you noticed that a website was confusing, do not start redesigning it. Instead, ask yourself why the confusion exists. Is the language unclear? Is the information disorganized? Did you expect one outcome but receive a different one? What is the website actually trying to solve for the person using it? Does it do that, or is it solving the wrong problem entirely?

Write your reframe in one or two sentences. Start with: "The real problem here is not X, it is Y." This does not need a perfect answer. The goal is to practice separating the symptom from the root cause.

Wrapping up

Summary

The UX mindset is not a checklist. It is a way of operating that prioritizes understanding problems over assuming them, and decisions over deliverables.

You will encounter "faster horses" thinking regularly throughout your career. Teams will rush to solutions. Stakeholders will ask for things that address the wrong problem. Research will be skipped in the name of speed.

The UX mindset is what allows you to recognize those moments, and make the case for a better approach. Not just because it is the right instinct, but because, as Ford and MacCready both demonstrated, it is what actually works.

In the next lesson, we will look at the other side of this: what happens when teams do engage users but fail to interpret what they are hearing and seeing.
