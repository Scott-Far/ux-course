Lesson 1.1 — Why deliverables fail and decisions succeed

The problem isn't the solution — it's how we think about the problem

There's a quote often attributed to Henry Ford: "If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses." It gets repeated endlessly in product and design circles as a justification for ignoring users and trusting gut instinct over research. The only problem? Ford almost certainly never said it.

But here's what's more interesting than the fact-check: even if he had said it, he would have been wrong. By 1908, the streets of London had over 50,000 horses producing mountains of waste daily. People weren't asking for faster horses — they were living in a public health crisis. The real need was obvious to anyone willing to look. Ford didn't succeed by ignoring his users. He succeeded by researching their actual conditions, understanding what they could afford, and engineering a solution that addressed the real problem at scale.

"The problem is we don't understand the problem." — Paul MacCready

What UX people actually get wrong

Most UX failures aren't failures of craft — they're failures of framing. Teams rush to solutions before they've accurately defined the problem. They build beautiful wireframes, run usability tests, and deliver polished reports. And then nothing changes, because the work was solving the wrong problem elegantly.

Consider how most technology projects fail. Research by Human Factors International estimates that 15% of IT projects worldwide are abandoned every year — representing roughly $150 billion wasted annually. The cause isn't bad engineering or poor visual design. It's teams that never properly understood what they were building or why.

The real cost of "faster horses" thinking When teams skip genuine user engagement, they don't just build the wrong thing — they build the wrong thing confidently, expensively, and at scale. The UX mindset is the antidote.

A better model: MacCready's lesson

In 1977, an aeronautical engineer named Paul MacCready won the Kremer Prize for human-powered flight — a challenge that had stumped design teams for 20 years. What did he do differently? He didn't build a better plane. He reframed the problem entirely.

Every team before him had been trying to build a plane that wouldn't crash. MacCready asked a different question: what if we built a plane that could crash and be rebuilt in hours instead of months? That single reframe changed everything. Where other teams iterated once a year, MacCready's team iterated daily. They failed faster, learned faster, and won.

This is the core of the UX mindset — and it's what this course is built around. Not just the methods and tools, but the thinking that makes them work: deep engagement with real users, honest diagnosis of real problems, and a willingness to test and revise rather than defend assumptions.

The deliverable trap

Here's where most UX education goes wrong, including most bootcamps: they teach you to produce deliverables. Personas. Wireframes. Journey maps. Usability reports. These artifacts aren't bad — but they're not the goal. They're evidence of thinking, not thinking itself.

When a deliverable becomes the goal, the work stops when the document is finished. Research is "done" when the report is written. Design is "done" when the wireframes are approved. Testing is "done" when the summary is emailed.

The question this course will train you to ask — at every stage, about every piece of work — is not "what should I produce?" but "what decision does this work need to support?"

  • A persona isn't a deliverable — it's a decision tool. It exists to answer: who are we designing for, and what do they need?
  • A wireframe isn't a deliverable — it's a hypothesis. It exists to be tested, revised, or discarded.
  • A usability test isn't a deliverable — it's a decision input. It exists to reduce uncertainty before committing to a direction.

What you'll be able to do differently

By the end of this course, you won't just know more methods. You'll think differently about what your work is for. You'll be able to walk into any project — no matter how vague the brief — and reframe it as a tractable decision problem. You'll know how to gather the right evidence, translate it into recommendations stakeholders can act on, and defend your decisions with something better than intuition.

That's the UX mindset. And it starts here.
