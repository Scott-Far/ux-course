In the last two lessons we established two things. First, assuming you know what users need without engaging them is a recipe for failure. Second, engaging users without knowing how to interpret what they tell you is equally dangerous.

So what does a better approach actually look like in practice?

Before we answer that, one more myth to put to rest.

One more quote that needs context
"We built the Mac for ourselves. We were the group of people who were going to judge whether it was great or not. We weren't going to go out and do market research." — Steve Jobs

This quote gets used the same way the Ford quote does — as justification for skipping user research because brilliant designers just know what's best.

But Jobs said something else that rarely gets quoted alongside it.

"Really great products come from melding two points of view — the technology point of view and the customer point of view. You need both. It takes a long time to pull out of customers what they really want, and it takes a long time to pull out of technology what it can really give." — Steve Jobs

Jobs was not ignoring his customers. He was doing exactly what we have been talking about — listening deeply, interpreting carefully, and not outsourcing design decisions to people who hadn't thought as hard about the problem as he had.

The lesson is the same one from Kawasaki. Don't give customers a more comfortable way to stand when sitting is what they actually need. Don't give them a faster horse when an affordable, easy to produce alternative solves the very real problem.

Good solutions do not come from ignoring what you see or assuming what is true. They come from knowing what is true by seeing it, experiencing it, and validating it before any solution is conceived.

Design Thinking

Henry Ford and Steve Jobs did not work alone. Neither should you.

The idea that meaningful solutions come from isolated expertise ignores how complex problems actually are. They require multiple perspectives, shared understanding, and a willingness to challenge assumptions so that a desire to be first does not end up as a costly mistake.

This is where Design Thinking comes in.

Design Thinking is a popular term that originated at Stanford University's School of Engineering as a way to tackle complex, ambiguous challenges. Its value comes from forcing teams to slow down, define a problem carefully, and address real constraints before committing to solutions.

Rather than jumping directly to execution, Design Thinking encourages teams to first clarify what problem they are actually solving, then explore a range of possible solutions and test those ideas with real people along the way. In doing so, Design Thinking exposes assumptions, surfaces risk, and encourages a willingness to iterate instead of rushing into solutions.

The three areas Design Thinking balances

At its core, Design Thinking requires teams to balance three key areas:

  • Technical Feasibility — What can realistically be built, given technical constraints, budgets, timelines, and organizational realities. A solution that cannot realistically be delivered is not a solution.
  • Business Viability — Does the solution support measurable outcomes and long-term return on investment? A solution that solves the user's problem but provides no value to the business is not sustainable.
  • Desirability and Need — Is the solution desirable and does it meaningfully address a problem uncovered through research?

Technology teams tend to build things that are feasible but not often needed by anyone outside the technology team. Business teams generally optimize for business accountability over usability. Design teams often create experiences that are desired but, when measured against the first two areas, are usually a much lower priority.

Design Thinking forces all three into the room at the same time.

The best solutions sit at the intersection of all three areas Feasible enough to build. Viable enough to sustain. Desirable and needed so people actually use them.
The five stages

Design Thinking is often represented in five stages. They are not a rigid sequence — in practice teams move back and forth between them — but they provide a useful map:

1
Empathize
Understand the people you are designing for. Observe them. Talk to them. Set aside your assumptions and engage with their actual experience.
2
Define
Synthesize what you learned into a clear problem statement. This is where "How might we" comes in — a tool that turns observations into design challenges.
3
Ideate
Generate possible solutions before committing to any of them.
4
Prototype
Create low-fidelity versions of the most promising ideas. The goal is to make your thinking tangible enough to test.
5
Test
Put your prototype in front of real people. Observe what happens. Learn. Revise.

Notice how this mirrors what we have been talking about across the first two lessons. Empathizing is listening and observing. Define is root cause analysis. Ideate is MacCready's approach to problem solving. Prototype and test are the iteration loop.

Design Thinking is not a new framework. It is a formal name given to an approach that good designers have always followed. Giving it a name makes it approachable and teachable to those who haven't looked at problems and solutions in this way, and gives them a map to follow.

"How might we"

The "How might we" statement is a Design Thinking tool that takes a potential problem or pain point and turns it into a design challenge — specific enough to guide decisions but open enough to allow for more than one solution.

A good "How might we" statement is not too broad and not too narrow.

  • Too broad: "How might we improve healthcare?"
  • Too narrow: "How might we add a search bar to the patient portal?"
  • Just right: "How might we help first-time patients navigate a hospital system without feeling overwhelmed or lost?"

Notice that the "just right" version is specific enough to point toward a real design challenge, but open enough that more than one solution could answer it. You could solve it with better signage, a guided onboarding flow, a simpler map, a staff escort system, or something else entirely. The statement does not prescribe the solution. It defines the problem.

Wrapping up

Summary

Design Thinking gives teams a structured way to do what good UX practitioners have always done — engage deeply, define clearly, explore broadly, and test before committing.

It is not a magic formula. It is a discipline. And like the UX mindset itself, it requires practice, thoughtfulness, and dedication to develop.
