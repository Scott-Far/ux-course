The three circles

⏱ ~15 min ✏️ 2 parts

What to do

Pick a digital product you use regularly and think about the experience of using it. Answer the questions below for each of the three circles from the lesson — Technical Feasibility, Business Viability, and Desirability and Need.

You do not need inside knowledge of the company or how it was built. Just describe what you observe and feel as a user. There are no wrong answers — this is about learning to look at a product through three different lenses at the same time.

Not sure what to pick? Choose one of these:

Bank mobile app Netflix Spotify Food delivery app Email app Google Maps Amazon Instagram
Example — Food delivery app

Technical Feasibility

The app works smoothly most of the time, but the live order tracking map often freezes or shows the driver going in the wrong direction. It feels like the tracking feature was added as a selling point but was not fully thought through. Instinctively it feels like it was hard to build and they shipped it before it was ready.

Business Viability

The app is clearly trying to get me to order more often — there are constant promotions, push notifications, and a rewards program. The company wants my money and my habitual use. The experience often feels designed for the company because the promotions and upsells get in the way of just finding what I want quickly.

Desirability and Need

It solves the problem of not wanting to cook or go out. But it does not fully solve it because I still spend too long browsing and never feel confident I found the best option. I wish it remembered my preferences better and surfaced the right restaurants without me having to search every time. If I were starting over I would design it around the user's history and preferences, not restaurant categories.

Part 2 — How might we

Write your problem statement

Using the product you just analyzed, identify the biggest problem you found. Not the symptom — the underlying cause. Then write a single "How might we" statement that captures it.

Here is the example continued from above:

"How might we help returning users find a meal they will enjoy without having to start from scratch every time they open the app?"

Notice that this statement is specific enough to point toward a real design challenge, but open enough that more than one solution could answer it — smarter recommendations, a favorites system, a quick reorder feature, or something else entirely.

If your statement only has one obvious answer, it is probably too narrow. If it could apply to almost any product, it is probably too broad.

How might we

Before you submit: does your statement leave room for more than one solution? If not, try making it slightly broader.
